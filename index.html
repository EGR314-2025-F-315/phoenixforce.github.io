Introduction

Why TerraGuard exists

Wildfires are becoming more common and more severe, and the biggest challenge is catching early hot spots before they grow. Many of the warning signs happen in remote terrain where people can’t check in person. TerraGuard is our answer: a rover that patrols, senses temperature, smoke, air quality, humidity, pressure, and shares live video so responders can react faster and safer.

Primary users are firefighters, fire-watch crews, and emergency responders. Secondary users include researchers and community partners who need simple, durable, and reliable data from the field. The rover raises alerts with LEDs and a speaker when readings cross thresholds, and everything is built to be understandable at a glance—even with gloves on.

Mission & Charter

Mission and charter

Team Charter

Build a reliable, realistic wildfire-detection prototype that works without public Wi‑Fi and surfaces clear, glove-friendly alerts. Hit measurable goals—low-latency warnings, accurate sensing, and consistent communication—and document trade-offs along the way.

Product Mission

Design a rugged, easy-to-use rover that senses early changes, sends dependable real-time information, and stays connected even when no public networks exist—so crews can decide faster and safer.

Decision-Making

Decision-making process

We brainstormed 100+ ideas and narrowed to 15: thermal drones, smoke sensors, LoRa mesh, rugged weather stations, swappable batteries, hybrid firefighting drones. The common thread was early wildfire detection with mobility and reliable comms. We chose a rover over a drone for cost, endurance, payload, and easier ops, then paired it with a simple HMI for clarity.

Instructor feedback pushed us to publish concept generation on-site, add callouts to sketches, cite AI imagery, and use interactive tabs/pop-ups. The biggest pivot was dropping a drone for a ground rover with mesh networking, HTTP control, and on-screen alerts—an approach that met requirements, stayed in budget, and remained original.

System Overview

Final system architecture

Two units work together: the Rover (ESP32‑CAM) and the Commander Screen (CrowPanel ESP32‑S3). The rover reads BME688, MQ-2, GPS, ultrasonic, MPU6050, drives motors via L298N, and hosts an AP. The screen joins that AP, polls for metrics, and shows MJPEG video via an LVGL interface with audio/LED alerts. Commands flow back over HTTP; safety checks stop motion when obstacles or high gas levels appear. Everything remains closed-loop on the rover’s AP for field reliability.

Design ideation details
Block Diagram

Block diagram rationale

We split the architecture into three modules—rover electronics, power, and screen/HMI—to clarify pins, rails, and comms. It satisfied EGR 314 requirements (actuator via L298N, I²C + analog sensing, microcontroller comms via UART/mesh) and guided PCB size, pin mapping, and voltage regulation. Ownership: Erjan (GPS + motors), Amanda + Danitza (BME688 + gas), Emonie (antennas), James (power), Vinny (screen + RX/TX).

Communication Sequence

Communication sequence

The commander polls telemetry and video over the rover’s AP, and sends drive commands back via HTTP. LEDs and audio cues fire locally on both units for immediate state feedback.

Component Selection

Component selection highlights

Parts were chosen for durability and fit: ESP32‑CAM for vision + control, CrowPanel ESP32‑S3 for HMI, BME688 + MQ-2 for air quality, GY‑NEO6MV2 for GPS, L298N for drive control, WS2812B LEDs for clear state, and a rugged 7.4V pack feeding 5V/3.3V rails.

Reflection

Reflection: lessons & next build

Key lessons and how we would evolve TerraGuard for Version 2.0.

Top 10 lessons

  • One ECAD stack only—KiCad vs Altium slowed merges.
  • Integration time dominates once subsystems work.
  • HTTP polling was simpler to debug than ESP-NOW.
  • WS2812 feedback made air-quality states obvious.
  • Budget PSRAM/frame buffers early for MJPEG.
  • Pin planning first—RF and camera lanes conflict fast.
  • MQ-2 calibration (Ro) mattered more than wiring.
  • Power budget reviews saved traces from overheating.
  • Collect assets early to avoid poster crunch.
  • Document endpoints to prevent “mystery 500s.”

Next: Version 2.0 ideas

  • Command channel screen → rover (drive + OTA).
  • Protobuf/CBOR payloads with versioning.
  • Double-buffered streaming with per-frame checksums.
  • Health pings to show link quality/jitter.
  • Layered code: comms, sensors, UI, drivers.
  • Thicker pours, shorter LED traces, RF keep-outs.
  • Add current sensing to validate the budget live.
  • Bring out UART/JTAG test pads.
  • Connector for future particulate or CO sensor.
